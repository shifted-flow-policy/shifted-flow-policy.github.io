Abstract

Diffusion models have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in generating high-quality data across various domains, including robotic action generation. However, existing diffusion-based policies often incur high computational costs due to iterative inference steps and lacks temporal bias during denoising. To address these challenges, we propose Adaptive Diffusion Policy (ADP), a novel approach that enhances both efficiency and performance in robotic action generation. ADP introduces two key components: Cascaded Diffusion, which enables efficient action synthesis by generating actions at each diffusion step while maintaining full process coverage, and Adaptive Rollout, which dynamically adjusts the noise scale based on the characteristics of the action sequence to balance performance and inference speed. Through comprehensive evaluations, ADP significantly outperforms current state-of-the-art diffusion-based policies in both performance and inference speed. Our method establishes a new benchmark for scalable and effective diffusion-based robotic action generation, offering a promising direction for future research in autonomous systems.

We evaluated ADP on a diverse set of tasks from the MimicGen benchmark, which features high-precision, contact-rich, and long-horizon manipulation scenarios. MimicGen is known for its challenging setups, including randomized initial states, object variations, and complex multi-step goals such as coffee preparation or multi-part assembly.

Our simulation results demonstrate that ADP performs robustly across these diverse settings, achieving strong task success rates while maintaining low inference latency. Notably, ADP successfully handles fine-grained manipulation and sequential planning challenges that are difficult for prior diffusion-based policies.

The videos above highlight the qualitative performance of ADP on several MimicGen tasks. These results validate ADP's effectiveness in generating accurate and temporally consistent actions even in highly dynamic and unpredictable environments.

To further illustrate the effectiveness of the proposed ADP compared to the DP, we present a visualization of the Push-T simulation results. As shown in the videos, we compare the action sequences generated by both models under identical conditions. Each video depicts action predictions from the same seed, allowing for a direct comparison of trajectory quality and temporal consistency.

In the case of ADP, the incorporation of temporal bias and a sliding-window-based noise removal strategy results in smoother and more stable action generation. ADP successfully leverages long-horizon forecasting by integrating Cascaded Diffusion and Adaptive Rollout, enabling consistent predictions far into the future.

Conversely, traditional policies exhibit temporal inconsistency and hesitates during short-horizon manipulations. Due to the absence of an adaptive mechanism, the model struggles to maintain coherent action sequences, particularly when predicting long-term movements. This hesitation results in momentary pauses or erratic motions, which are not observed in ADP.

These findings underscore the advantages of the proposed ADP framework in managing sequential dependencies, reducing cumulative noise, and enhancing long-term action prediction, ultimately leading to a more stable and efficient robotic motion planning algorithm.
