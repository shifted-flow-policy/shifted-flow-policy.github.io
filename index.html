Abstract

Imitation learning for robotics often uses action chunking to mitigate the compounding errors associated with autoregressive policies. By predicting multiple future actions simultaneously, action chunking limits the accumulation of errors but introduces new difficulties. In particular, it relies on outdated observations to predict future actions, which can lead to inaccuracies. In this study, we propose Shifted Flow Policy (SFP), a simple yet effective alternative to action chunking. The SFP reparameterizes time by linearly shifting the time steps for future actions, thereby capturing the natural increase in uncertainty over time. This formulation allows each predicted action to be conditioned on up-to-date observations. Experimental results on the Push-T and MimicGen benchmarks demonstrate that SFP outperforms state-of-the-art action chunking methods across a variety of manipulation tasks by achieving higher success rates and faster inference. These findings suggest that shifted flow provides a robust and practical alternative to action chunking in visuomotor policy learning.

We evaluated SFP on a diverse set of tasks from the MimicGen benchmark, which features high-precision, contact-rich, and long-horizon manipulation scenarios. MimicGen is known for its challenging setups, including randomized initial states, object variations, and complex multi-step goals such as coffee preparation or multi-part assembly.

Our simulation results demonstrate that SFP performs robustly across these diverse settings, achieving strong task success rates while maintaining low inference latency. Notably, SFP successfully handles fine-grained manipulation and sequential planning challenges that are difficult for prior diffusion-based policies.

The videos above highlight the qualitative performance of SFP on several MimicGen tasks. These results validate SFP's effectiveness in generating accurate and temporally consistent actions even in highly dynamic and unpredictable environments.

To further illustrate the effectiveness of the proposed SFP compared to the DP, we present a visualization of the Push-T simulation results. As shown in the videos, we compare the action sequences generated by both models under identical conditions. Each video depicts action predictions from the same seed, allowing for a direct comparison of trajectory quality and temporal consistency.

In the case of SFP, the incorporation of temporal bias and a sliding-window-based noise removal strategy results in smoother and more stable action generation. SFP successfully leverages long-horizon forecasting by integrating Cascaded Diffusion and Adaptive Rollout, enabling consistent predictions far into the future.

Conversely, traditional policies exhibit temporal inconsistency and hesitates during short-horizon manipulations. Due to the absence of an adaptive mechanism, the model struggles to maintain coherent action sequences, particularly when predicting long-term movements. This hesitation results in momentary pauses or erratic motions, which are not observed in SFP.

These findings underscore the advantages of the proposed SFP framework in managing sequential dependencies, reducing cumulative noise, and enhancing long-term action prediction, ultimately leading to a more stable and efficient robotic motion planning algorithm.
