Overview of the Proposed Shifted Flow Policy (SFP) Architecture: (Left) ction chunking predicts a fixed length sequence of actions that all share the same timestep and are executed in an open-loop manner by relying on a single observation. In contrast,(Right) shifted flow assigns linearly increasing timesteps to each action within the prediction window to model future uncertainty by enabling updated observations to condition each sampling step.

Sampling process of shifted flow

(Left) Shifted Timesteps

The timestep \( t \) is continuously shifted as the action index \( k \) increases. By shifting \( t \) at the same rate as that at which it increases, a diagonal pattern emerges where noisy black actions are pushed towards white valid actions.

(Middle) Initialize Window

Following the shifted timesteps \( t_w \), the window can be initialized from noise where the final actions have timesteps:

\[ t_w = \left[1, \frac{W-1}{W}, \cdots, \frac{2}{W}, \frac{1}{W}\right] \]

(Right) Shifting Window

After retrieving the first action to execute on the environment, the window shifts to the right and adds noise to the end as a new observation \( O_1 \) is observed from the environment. This process is repeated to generate more actions.

Abstract

Imitation learning for robotics often uses action chunking to mitigate the compounding errors associated with autoregressive policies. By predicting multiple future actions simultaneously, action chunking limits the accumulation of errors but introduces new difficulties. In particular, it relies on outdated observations to predict future actions, which can lead to inaccuracies. In this study, we propose Shifted Flow Policy (SFP), a simple yet effective alternative to action chunking. The SFP reparameterizes time by linearly shifting the time steps for future actions, thereby capturing the natural increase in uncertainty over time. This formulation allows each predicted action to be conditioned on up-to-date observations. Experimental results on the Push-T and MimicGen benchmarks demonstrate that SFP outperforms state-of-the-art action chunking methods across a variety of manipulation tasks by achieving higher success rates and faster inference. These findings suggest that shifted flow provides a robust and practical alternative to action chunking in visuomotor policy learning.

We evaluated SFP on a diverse set of tasks from the MimicGen benchmark, which features high-precision, contact-rich, and long-horizon manipulation scenarios. MimicGen is known for its challenging setups, including randomized initial states, object variations, and complex multi-step goals such as coffee preparation or multi-part assembly.

Our simulation results demonstrate that SFP performs robustly across these diverse settings, achieving strong task success rates while maintaining low inference latency. Notably, SFP successfully handles fine-grained manipulation and sequential planning challenges that are difficult for prior diffusion-based policies.

The videos above highlight the qualitative performance of SFP on several MimicGen tasks. These results validate SFP's effectiveness in generating accurate and temporally consistent actions even in highly dynamic and unpredictable environments.

To demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed Shifted Flow Policy (SFP), we visualize action rollouts on the Push-T task in simulation. Both policies are initialized with the same random seed to ensure direct comparability.

SFP leverages shifted time steps and a sliding window formulation to model increasing uncertainty over long horizons. Instead of predicting a fixed-length chunk of actions at a constant timestep—as done in traditional action chunking methods—SFP progressively generates future actions with incrementally shifted time indices. This enables smoother transitions and avoids reliance on outdated observations.

As seen in the right animation, SFP produces a fluid and continuous trajectory that pushes the T-shaped block toward the goal with high stability. The motion remains consistent even as the object rotates or deviates slightly, indicating strong temporal coherence.

In contrast, the baseline Diffusion Policy exhibits occasional pauses and trajectory jitter, particularly when encountering ambiguous intermediate states. These behaviors arise from its open-loop execution of fixed action chunks, where actions sampled early on can become outdated before being executed.

Overall, SFP not only improves the trajectory quality, but also demonstrates robustness in contact-rich settings such as Push-T by mitigating error accumulation and maintaining a stable prediction pipeline across long sequences.
